00 · Executive overview

Mobile Networks from the Antenna Inward

A mobile network is a distributed real-time signal-processing machine. Its scarcest resource is radio spectrum; its hardest constraint is physics; its most expensive mistake is deploying the wrong thing in the wrong place. This module builds your understanding from the electromagnetic wave up to the AI-driven control loops of 6G.

01 · End-to-end architecture

From a Phone to the Internet: the Whole Machine

Intuition

Think of the network as three concentric machines. The RAN converts unreliable radio waves into reliable bit pipes — it fights physics. The transport network hauls those bits across fiber and microwave — it fights distance and cost. The core network decides who you are, what you may do, and where your packets should exit — it fights complexity and scale. Around all three sits management and orchestration, the nervous system that configures, monitors, and increasingly learns.

The path of one packet (uplink)

  1. UE: Your phone's application writes to a socket → IP packet → PDCP ciphering → RLC segmentation → MAC multiplexing → PHY encodes it onto an OFDM waveform and transmits when the scheduler grants it resources.
  2. Radio Unit (RU): The antenna captures ~microvolts. The RU amplifies (LNA), filters, digitizes, and performs low-PHY functions (FFT, beamforming combining in a 7.2x split), then streams frequency-domain IQ samples over fronthaul (eCPRI, ~25 Gbps class, <100 µs latency budget).
  3. Distributed Unit (DU): Runs high-PHY (channel estimation, equalization, LDPC decoding), MAC (HARQ, scheduling), and RLC. This is the real-time heart — its control loops run every slot (0.5 ms at 30 kHz SCS).
  4. Centralized Unit (CU): Runs PDCP/SDAP (user plane) and RRC (control plane) over midhaul (F1 interface, ~1–10 ms tolerable). One CU typically serves many DUs.
  5. Core (5GC): The UPF forwards the user plane; AMF/SMF handle mobility and sessions over backhaul (NG interface). The UPF may sit in a regional data center — or at the edge cloud a few km away when latency matters.
  6. Internet / cloud: The UPF's N6 interface is the door to the world.

Engineering reality: latency budgets shape geography

The functional split you choose shapes where boxes can physically live. For low-layer splits such as 7.2x, the DU usually needs to be close to the RU — often within single-digit to a few tens of kilometres — because the fronthaul one-way latency budget (driven by HARQ/scheduling timing) plus packetization, switching, sync distribution and vendor implementation eat the available margin quickly. PDCP tolerates milliseconds, so the CU can typically sit 100+ km away in a regional cloud. Very low-latency use cases may pull a local UPF to the edge, but end-to-end latency depends on radio scheduling, processing delay, transport, application location and the actual reliability/latency target — not fiber propagation alone (~5 µs/km is only one term in the budget). The right question is rarely "is it 1 ms?" but "does this service truly need sub-5 ms or sub-1 ms, and at what reliability?"

Trade-off Centralize functions → pooling gains, easier coordination, cheaper operations. Distribute functions → lower latency, lower transport cost, better survivability. Every architecture decision in this module is a point on this axis.
02 · RAN fundamentals

What the RAN Actually Does

Intuition

The RAN's job is to run a fair, fast auction for a hostile shared medium. Thousands of devices want the same spectrum at the same time, the channel to each of them changes every few milliseconds, and users move. The RAN solves this with a control loop: measure the channel → decide who transmits what, where, and how robustly → transmit → learn from errors → repeat, 2,000 times per second.

Anatomy of a cell site

  • Cell site: the physical location — tower, rooftop, or street pole — with power, cooling, transport, and antennas.
  • Sector: most macro sites split 360° into three 120° sectors. Each sector is one or more cells (one per carrier frequency).
  • Antenna: passive panels (fed by an external radio) or active antenna units (AAU) where 32–64 transceivers are integrated behind the radome for massive MIMO.
  • Radio unit: converts between digital baseband samples and RF watts. Contains PAs, LNAs, filters, DACs/ADCs. Consumes most of the site's power.
  • Baseband unit (BBU/DU): the digital signal-processing engine — scheduling, coding, MIMO processing.

03 · Generations

4G → 5G → 5G-Advanced → 6G

Intuition

Each generation is best understood by what it made programmable. 4G made the transport of IP packets programmable (flat, all-IP architecture). 5G made the network itself programmable (service-based core, slicing, functional splits). 5G-Advanced makes the network learnable (AI/ML in the air interface and control loops). 6G aims to make it self-designing and sensing — the network as a distributed AI and a distributed radar at once.

The 5G deployment path: NSA vs SA

NSA (Non-Standalone, Option 3x): NR carriers anchor to an LTE cell and the 4G EPC core via EN-DC (E-UTRA–NR Dual Connectivity). The LTE anchor carries the RRC control plane and mobility; the user plane is split so traffic can flow over LTE, NR, or both. This let operators light up 5G radios years before 5G cores were ready — which is exactly why early 5G often felt like "faster 4G" rather than a new architecture: the throughput jumped, but the network underneath was still EPC with an NR speed booster bolted on.

SA (Standalone, Option 2): the gNB connects directly to the 5GC, with NR carrying the control plane. This is where 5G stops being a radio upgrade and becomes a platform: native network slicing, lower core-path latency, VoNR, RedCap, better private/enterprise networking, cleaner automation hooks (NWDAF, exposure), and a clean runway to 5G-Advanced. The trade is that SA is slower and more complex to roll out — a new core, new interworking, VoNR maturity, and device support all have to land together.

Operator implication: most operators that launched NSA first now run a multi-year NSA→SA migration. NSA is best read as a transitional configuration that monetized 5G radio early; SA is the long-term platform. The two coexist for years, and devices/features are gated by which core a user is actually camped on.

04 · 5G RAN architecture

Disaggregating the gNodeB: CU, DU, RU and the Splits

Intuition

A 4G eNodeB was one box doing everything. 5G asked: which functions must be near the antenna, and which merely want to be? The answer produced a three-way cut of the gNB. Functions with microsecond deadlines (RF, FFT, beamforming) → RU at the antenna. Functions with slot-level deadlines (scheduling, HARQ, LDPC) → DU nearby. Functional splits: the bandwidth–latency–intelligence triangle

3GPP enumerated splits 1–8. Two dominate in practice:

  • Option 2 (PDCP/RLC): CU↔DU. Low midhaul bandwidth (≈ user throughput), relaxed latency (~ms). Enables CU centralization.
  • Option 7.2x (intra-PHY): DU↔RU. The compromise that made Open RAN viable: the RU does FFT/iFFT, CP handling, and (for massive MIMO) beamforming — so fronthaul carries per-layer frequency-domain samples instead of per-antenna time-domain samples. That collapses a 64T64R fronthaul from >200 Gbps (split 8/CPRI) to ~10–25 Gbps, at the price of putting some intelligence (and vendor differentiation) into the RU.

05 · Synchronization & timing

The Invisible Clock That Holds TDD Together

Intuition

Every base station needs to agree on what frequency to run at and — for TDD — exactly when its slots start. Frequency sync keeps carriers from drifting; phase/time sync keeps the whole network's uplink and downlink boundaries aligned to a common instant. In FDD this is merely important. In TDD it is existential: neighbours share one band and take turns, so if their clocks disagree, one cell's downlink blast lands right in another cell's uplink receive window. That is cross-link interference (CLI), and it can wreck a whole cluster.

Three kinds of synchronization

TypeWhat it alignsWho needs itTypical source
FrequencyCarrier frequency / clock rate (ppb-level)All cellular (avoid carrier drift, inter-carrier interference)SyncE, PTP, GNSS
PhaseSlot/symbol boundary alignment (µs-level)TDD, mMIMO, CoMP, positioningPTP (G.8275.1), GNSS
TimeAbsolute time-of-day (ToD) referenceTDD networks, features needing common epochGNSS, PTP grandmaster

The delivery mechanisms

  • GNSS (GPS/Galileo/…): a per-site receiver gives absolute time and phase directly. Cheap and accurate, but vulnerable — indoor/urban-canyon sites can't see satellites, and jamming/spoofing is a growing operational risk.
  • PTP (IEEE 1588, ITU-T G.8275.1): Precision Time Protocol distributes phase/time over the packet transport using boundary/transparent clocks at every hop. This is how sync reaches sites that can't rely on GNSS — but every switch in the path must support PTP, or timing error accumulates.
  • SyncE (ITU-T G.8262): Synchronous Ethernet carries a physical-layer frequency reference in the Ethernet clock. It delivers frequency, not phase — usually paired with PTP (SyncE for frequency stability, PTP for phase).
  • Holdover: when the reference is lost, the site coasts on a local oscillator (often an OCXO). Good holdover buys minutes-to-hours before phase drifts out of the TDD budget; a cheap oscillator drifts out fast — and then CLI begins.
Why sync failure is catastrophic in TDDIn a TDD network, neighbouring cells must align their uplink and downlink timing. If one cell is transmitting downlink while a neighbouring cell is trying to receive uplink, the strong downlink transmission can overwhelm the neighbour's sensitive uplink receiver. That is cross-link interference: a timing fault that masquerades as a coverage or interference fault. A single site with failed GNSS and weak holdover can desensitize every cell around it — which is why sync alarms are treated as high-severity.

Why this matters more now

Fronthaul makes the timing problem stricter. Under low-layer splits (7.2x), the RU depends on the DU/network for a tight timing reference delivered over the fronthaul — and in Open RAN that timing now crosses vendor boundaries, so RU, DU, transport and grandmaster must interoperate on PTP profiles and holdover behaviour. mMIMO reciprocity, CoMP and positioning all tighten the phase budget further. Emerging SBFD (sub-band full duplex) and dynamic-TDD schemes raise the stakes again, because they deliberately blur the UL/DL time boundary and lean even harder on precise, network-wide synchronization to keep CLI manageable.

06 · NR air interface

5G NR: One Radio, Many Personalities

Intuition

LTE was one fixed radio design. NR is a family of radio designs parameterized by numerology µ: subcarrier spacing SCS = 15 · 2µ kHz. Wider subcarriers → shorter symbols → shorter slots → lower latency and better phase-noise/Doppler tolerance (good for mmWave and speed), but a shorter cyclic prefix → less tolerance to delay spread (bad for big macro cells). The waveform bends to the band and the service.

Frame structure and resources

  • Frame 10 ms → 10 subframes → 2µ slots each → 14 OFDM symbols per slot.
  • Resource element (RE): 1 subcarrier × 1 symbol — the atom. Resource block (RB): 12 subcarriers. At 30 kHz SCS, 100 MHz ⇒ 273 RBs ⇒ 3,276 subcarriers.
  • Mini-slots (2/4/7 symbols): let URLLC traffic start mid-slot instead of waiting — the key to sub-ms latency.
  • BWP (bandwidth parts): a UE can operate in a narrow slice of the carrier — saving IoT/RedCap device power.

Initial access, in one breath

UE scans the synchronization raster → detects an SSB (in FR2, waits for the best of up to 64 swept beams) → decodes MIB/SIB1 → sends PRACH preamble on the resources tied to its chosen beam → 4-step (or 2-step) RACH exchange → RRC connection. From the very first message, NR is beam-centric: the network knows your best beam before it knows your identity.

HARQ: the reliability engine

Every transport block gets a fast ACK/NACK. On NACK the gNB retransmits different redundancy versions of the same LDPC codeword, and the UE soft-combines them (incremental redundancy). Operating point: first transmission targets ~10% BLER deliberately — pushing aggressive MCS and letting HARQ mop up is more spectrally efficient than being conservative. Latency cost per retransmission: a few slots; URLLC avoids it via lower target BLER and repetitions instead.

TDD vs FDD in one paragraph FDD: separate paired bands for UL/DL — simultaneous both ways, simple, great for coverage low-band, but the paired spectrum is scarce. TDD: one band, time-shared — all wide mid/high bands are TDD. TDD gives channel reciprocity (UL sounding reveals the DL channel — the enabler of massive MIMO precoding) and flexible UL:DL ratios, but costs guard periods, needs network-wide slot synchronization to avoid cross-link interference, and typically gives DL ~75% of airtime — creating the structural UL bottleneck that Section 16 (uplink) and 5G-A's SBFD try to fix.
07 · Spectrum & propagation

Spectrum: the Real Estate of Wireless

Intuition

Frequency buys you bandwidth; wavelength buys you reach. As carrier frequency rises, (i) free-space loss referenced to isotropic antennas grows as f², because a fixed-gain antenna's effective aperture shrinks as λ²; (ii) diffraction around obstacles weakens; (iii) penetration through walls and foliage collapses; (iv) at sub-THz, molecular absorption (O₂ at 60 GHz, H₂O at 183 GHz) adds dB/km. The compensation is that short wavelengths let you pack huge arrays into small panels — beamforming gain claws back link budget, but only along a narrow, blockable line.

FSPL(dB) = 32.45 + 20·log₁₀(f_MHz) + 20·log₁₀(d_km) 3.5 GHz vs 700 MHz at same distance: +14 dB 28 GHz vs 700 MHz: +32 dB (before walls, rain, foliage) Typical exterior wall: 700 MHz ~10 dB · 3.5 GHz ~20 dB · 28 GHz ~40+ dB (often blocked)
Say "power" precisely"Power" is not one number — a link budget needs the right one in the right place:
  • UE max Tx power — the device PA ceiling, typically ~23 dBm (200 mW) for a handset (power class 3). Fixed and small.
  • gNB conducted RF power — power at the PA/antenna port, before antenna gain.
  • PA output — raw amplifier output, from which conducted power is derived after filter/feeder loss.
  • Antenna gain — directional focusing (dBi); for a massive-MIMO panel this includes beamforming array gain.
  • EIRP = conducted power + antenna gain − losses. This is what actually reaches the UE, and it is regulator-capped.
  • Total site power — DC/AC watts the site draws (radios + baseband + cooling): an energy quantity, not a link-budget term.
Because the gNB's EIRP dwarfs the UE's ~23 dBm, the two directions are asymmetric: downlink can shout, the uplink can only whisper. This is why uplink is frequently the coverage-limiting direction even when downlink RSRP looks fine (see §16).

Channel effects you must model

  • Shadowing (slow fading): log-normal, σ ≈ 6–8 dB — buildings and terrain. Sets the coverage margin.
  • Fast fading: multipath interference, changes over ~λ/2 of movement — what link adaptation chases.
  • Delay spread: path-length differences (~0.1–5 µs) → sets CP length → constrains numerology per environment.
  • Doppler spread: f_D = v·f/c. At 3.5 GHz, 120 km/h ⇒ ~390 Hz ⇒ channel decorrelates in ~1 ms — CSI aging becomes the enemy of precoding.

Spectrum machinery

  • Carrier aggregation: bond carriers across bands (e.g., 700 MHz anchor + 3.5 GHz capacity): coverage of the low band, throughput of the sum.
  • Dynamic Spectrum Sharing (DSS): LTE and NR share one FDD carrier, scheduler-multiplexed per ms. Migration tool, ~10–20% capacity overhead — a bridge, not a destination.
  • Refarming: retire 2G/3G, re-license the band to NR — where most "new" 5G FDD spectrum actually comes from.

08 · Antennas & massive MIMO

Steering Energy Through Space

Intuition

An antenna array is an interference machine. Feed the same signal to N elements with per-element phase shifts, and the wavefronts add constructively in one chosen direction and cancel elsewhere. Double the elements → +3 dB gain and roughly half the beamwidth. With independent radio chains per element (digital beamforming), the array can form many simultaneous beams — and that unlocks spatial multiplexing: serving several users on the same time-frequency resources (MU-MIMO), multiplying cell capacity without new spectrum.

Uniform linear array, N elements, spacing d, steering angle θ₀: AF(θ) = | Σₙ exp( j·2π·(d/λ)·n·(sinθ − sinθ₀) ) | / N Gain ≈ 10·log₁₀(N) dBi (+ element gain) · HPBW ≈ 102°/N (d=λ/2)

Engineering reality

  • Passive vs active: passive panels + remote radio (LTE style) vs Active Antenna Units — 32T32R/64T64R transceivers integrated behind the radome. AAUs enable per-element digital control but weigh 20–40 kg and dominate site power.
  • mMIMO modes: Beamforming gain (coverage — one strong beam), SU-MIMO (up to 4–8 layers to one phone), MU-MIMO (8–16 layers across users — the capacity multiplier, needs users spatially separated and accurate CSI).
  • Getting CSI: TDD reciprocity via SRS (best — full channel knowledge) vs FDD codebook feedback (PMI — quantized, coarser). This is why mMIMO shines on TDD mid-band.
  • FR2 beam management: analog/hybrid arrays sweep SSB beams (P-1), refine with CSI-RS (P-2), track UE movement (P-3). Beam failure → recovery procedure. Every FR2 link is a fragile pencil of energy.
  • Classical geometry still matters: mechanical/electrical tilt trades coverage vs overshoot interference; azimuth and sectorization (3→6 sectors) and cell splitting remain the cheapest capacity tools before new spectrum.

09 · Baseband processing

Baseband: Where Bits Become Waveforms

Intuition

Baseband is the assembly line between IP packets and RF samples. Downlink is a protection and shaping pipeline: take a scheduled block of bits, armor it against errors (coding), pack it densely (modulation), spread it across antennas (MIMO), and paint it onto the time-frequency grid (OFDM). Uplink is the mirror run in reverse — but much harder, because the receiver must first discover what the channel did to the signal before it can undo it.

Downlink stages worth internalizing

  • Channel coding — LDPC for data: chosen over Turbo for parallel-hardware decoding at 10+ Gbps and a fine-grained rate-matching design (two base graphs; puncture/shorten to any rate). Polar for control: optimal at the short block lengths of DCI/UCI where LDPC is weak.
  • Rate matching: the circular buffer that turns one mother code into any code rate and generates HARQ redundancy versions RV0–3.
  • Precoding: multiply layers by matrix W to pre-compensate the channel — the mathematical heart of mMIMO. Regularized zero-forcing/MMSE weights suppress inter-user interference in MU-MIMO.
  • Digital front-end (DFE): the unsung 20% — crest-factor reduction (OFDM's ~10 dB PAPR would murder PA efficiency) and digital predistortion (linearize the PA so you can drive it hot). DFE quality largely decides site energy consumption.

Uplink is the hard direction

The gNB must estimate the channel from sparse DMRS while the signal is 20–30 dB weaker than DL (phone PA ≈ 0.2 W vs gNB 100+ W), separate co-scheduled MU-MIMO users whose signals arrive superimposed (MMSE or successive interference cancellation), and finish LDPC decoding within the HARQ deadline. Uplink DSP dominates DU compute budgets.

10 · RF hardware reality

Where the Math Meets the Metal

Intuition

Baseband algorithms assume a clean, linear radio. Real radios are none of those things. The power amplifier distorts, the oscillator jitters, the converters quantize, and the receiver adds noise. Every one of these impairments sets a ceiling that no amount of clever DSP can fully lift — which is why baseband and RF cannot be designed in isolation. The most common way that a beautiful 256-QAM plan dies in the field is an RF impairment nobody budgeted for.

Transmitter-side reality

  • PA non-linearity: the power amplifier is only linear well below saturation. Push it for efficiency and it clips, splattering energy into neighbouring channels and distorting your own constellation.
  • PAPR (peak-to-average power ratio): OFDM waveforms have large peaks. To avoid clipping those peaks, you back the PA off — wasting efficiency. High PAPR is a core reason uplink historically used DFT-s-OFDM (lower PAPR → more usable UE Tx power).
  • DPD (digital predistortion): pre-distort the signal with the PA's inverse so the output comes out linear. Lets you run the PA hotter (more efficient) while meeting emission limits — but DPD needs compute, calibration and feedback.
  • CFR (crest factor reduction): deliberately clip/shape peaks to cut PAPR before the PA, trading a little in-band EVM for efficiency and ACLR headroom.
  • ACLR (adjacent channel leakage ratio): how much power spills into neighbours' channels — regulator-limited. Poor linearity → ACLR violation → you interfere with the operator next door.
  • EVM (error vector magnitude): how far received symbols land from their ideal constellation points. High-order modulation (256/1024-QAM) demands very low EVM; a few percent EVM quietly caps you at 64-QAM regardless of SINR.

Receiver-side reality

  • Noise figure (NF): the receiver's own added noise. Every dB of NF is a dB of sensitivity lost — it directly shifts the uplink coverage edge.
  • Phase noise: oscillator instability that smears subcarriers. It worsens sharply at high frequency, which is one reason mmWave/sub-THz high-order modulation is hard.
  • IQ imbalance: gain/phase mismatch between the I and Q paths creates an image that raises the EVM floor.
  • ADC/DAC resolution: finite bits quantize the signal. Wide bandwidth + many antennas make high-resolution, high-rate converters power-hungry and expensive — a real constraint behind mMIMO and (especially) sub-THz front-ends.
  • Blocking & desensitization: a strong nearby signal (even out-of-band) can saturate the front-end and desensitize the receiver, collapsing sensitivity even where the wanted signal is fine.
  • Thermal limits: PAs and converters generate heat; sustained peak power is bounded by cooling. This ties RF performance to the site's energy and thermal design.
Why baseband can't ignore RFHigher-order modulation needs a lower EVM floor; wider bandwidth needs flatter response, faster converters and better phase noise; more antennas multiply converter count and calibration burden. So the "just use 1024-QAM over 400 MHz on 64 layers" plan runs straight into PA linearity, converter power, phase noise and thermal limits. Link adaptation (§11) is, in part, the scheduler negotiating with these hardware ceilings in real time.

12 · MAC & scheduling

The Scheduler: 2,000 Auctions per Second

Intuition

Every slot, the MAC scheduler holds an auction for the RB grid. Each user's "bid" is shaped by its channel quality, its QoS contract, how long its data has waited, and how much it has already received. The classic genius move is proportional fair (PF): rank users by instantaneous rate / average rate. It rides each user's fading peaks (multi-user diversity — cell throughput actually grows with more users) while the denominator guarantees starving users eventually win.

PF metric: argmaxᵤ rᵤ(t) / R̄ᵤ(t) R̄ᵤ ← (1−1/T)·R̄ᵤ + (1/T)·served Round robin: fairness in time, blind to channel → wastes capacity Max C/I: argmaxᵤ rᵤ(t) → max cell tput, starves the edge QoS-aware: metric × delay/GBR multipliers; URLLC preempts via mini-slots (may puncture eMBB mid-flight — Rel-16 pre-emption indication)

The plumbing around the decision

  • Buffer Status Reports (UL) and queue depths (DL) tell the scheduler who has data.
  • Scheduling Request → grant round trip costs latency; configured grants pre-allocate UL resources for URLLC to skip it.
  • QoS (5QI) maps flows to priority, delay budget, and GBR/non-GBR treatment.
  • Slicing at the MAC: a slice becomes a resource-partitioning policy — e.g., "slice A guaranteed 30% of RBs, may borrow idle capacity." The RIC can retune these ratios in near-real time.

The core procedures

  • Registration: the UE announces itself to the core (via NAS), authenticates, and is assigned a registration area.
  • Tracking areas & paging: the network knows the UE only to tracking-area granularity when idle. To reach it, it pages across the area. Too-large areas → heavy paging load; too-small → frequent updates. This is a classic mobility/signalling trade-off.

So the clean layering has a feedback loop — low-layer radio quality (PHY) drives control-plane decisions (RRC), which reconfigure the low layers again. Most "network" behaviour users notice (attach time, wake-up latency, dropped calls on a corridor) is really this control-plane machinery reacting to radio conditions.
14 · Mobility & handover

Staying Connected at 120 km/h

Intuition

Handover is a bet placed under noise. Signal strength differences between cells fluctuate ±8 dB from shadowing, so "neighbor is stronger" for one instant means nothing. The network therefore demands the neighbor be stronger by a margin (hysteresis) for a duration (time-to-trigger). Set them too tight → ping-pong (bouncing between cells, signaling storms). Too loose → the UE clings to a dying cell until the radio link fails → drop. The optimum shifts with speed, which is why one static parameter set can never win — and why mobility was among the first AI/RIC use cases.

A3 event: RSRP_neighbor > RSRP_serving + Offset + Hysteresis for TTT ms Idle mode: cell selection/reselection (UE-driven, S/R criteria) Connected: measurement report → HO decision → prep over Xn → RRC Reconfig → RACH to target → path switch. Interruption ≈ 30–50 ms (DAPS/LTM in Rel-17/18 push toward ~0 ms)

The mobility family

  • Intra-frequency HO: same carrier — the common case; measurements are free.
  • Inter-frequency HO: requires measurement gaps (UE retunes away, costing throughput); used for load balancing and coverage layering (3.5 GHz → 700 MHz at the edge).
  • Inter-RAT: NR ↔ LTE fallback (e.g., voice, coverage edges).
  • Beam-level mobility (FR2): beam switches within a cell handled at L1/L2 — no RRC, milliseconds not tens of ms. 6G thinking pushes ever more mobility down to this cheap layer (and cell-free architectures aim to abolish the hard cell boundary entirely).
  • Dual connectivity / CA mobility: anchor stability (low band) + fast secondary changes (mid/mmWave) — split the reliability problem from the capacity problem.
  • MRO: mobility robustness optimization — the SON/rApp loop that tunes offsets per cell pair from HO failure statistics.

The Iron Triangle of RAN Design

Intuition

In a noise-limited network you fight physics; in an interference-limited network you fight yourself. Dense urban grids are firmly the latter: most of the "noise" degrading a user's SINR is the operator's own neighboring cells. That's why simply adding power or sites eventually stops helping — every cell you add is both a server and an interferer.

16 · Uplink: the limiting direction

The Direction Everyone Forgets Until It Breaks

Intuition

Marketing measures networks by download speed, so uplink gets designed as an afterthought — until FWA, video calls, live streaming, cloud backup and enterprise apps make it the bottleneck. The root cause is a power asymmetry the physics won't let you escape: the base station can shout, the handset can only whisper.

The asymmetry in one paragraphThe UE is power-limited — a handset (power class 3) tops out around 23 dBm (200 mW) of conducted Tx power, spread across whatever bandwidth it's granted. The gNB has far higher conducted RF power, large antenna gain and correspondingly high EIRP (see §07). So even when downlink RSRP looks healthy, the uplink may not reach the site — downlink coverage does not prove uplink coverage.

Why uplink runs out of headroom first

  • Path loss is symmetric, power is not. The same path loss applies both ways, but the UE has ~an order of magnitude less power and gain to fight it. The result: an uplink coverage hole inside an area with fine downlink signal.
  • Power headroom (PHR): the UE reports how much Tx power it has left. When a cell-edge or indoor UE hits its power ceiling, it can't take a higher MCS or a wider grant no matter how good the scheduler is — it is simply out of watts.
  • Band choice hurts uplink most. Mid-band (3.5 GHz) has worse propagation than low-band; indoors this bites the power-limited uplink harder than the high-EIRP downlink.
  • TDD slot scarcity. TDD spends most airtime on downlink (often ~75–80%), so the uplink gets fewer symbols and the UE is power-limited within them — a double penalty. This is the structural bottleneck that dynamic-TDD and SBFD try to relieve.
  • Uplink MIMO is limited. Handsets have few Tx chains and tight power budgets, so uplink spatial multiplexing is far more constrained than the rich downlink mMIMO story.

Why some traffic stresses uplink differently

Ordinary mobile broadband is download-heavy, so networks dimensioned for it look fine on paper. But FWA (home broadband over cellular), video upload, live streaming/creator traffic, video calls and enterprise/industrial uplink invert the ratio — they sustain high uplink for long periods from fixed or indoor locations. That is exactly where the power-limited, TDD-starved uplink runs out first, and where SUL, added site density, uplink-favourable TDD patterns and careful power control earn their keep.

Practical takeawayA user may see good downlink signal, but the cell can still be uplink-limited because the UE cannot transmit enough power back to the site — especially at mid-band or indoors. When diagnosing "slow" cells, check PUSCH SINR, power headroom and UL BLER, not just RSRP (see §17). Downlink RSRP alone never proves the uplink is healthy.

How the pieces connect

  • Distinguish coverage from quality. RSRP answers "is there enough signal?"; SINR/RSRQ/CQI/BLER answer "is that signal usable?" A cell can have strong RSRP and terrible SINR (interference-limited) — the fixes are completely different.

19 · AI/ML in the RAN

From Hand-Tuned Rules to Learned Control

Intuition

Classical RAN algorithms are hand-designed policies over simplified models. AI enters wherever (a) the real system is messier than the model, and (b) there is abundant data and a measurable reward. Two distinct frontiers matter — don't conflate them:

  • DEPLOYEDAI around the RAN (network automation): traffic prediction, anomaly detection, parameter optimization — rApps/xApps acting on 100 ms–days timescales. This is real and in production today (energy, MRO, load management).

The honest caveats

AI's advantage degrades exactly where you'd predict: when channel coherence collapses (predictions age out), when the deployment differs from training data (generalization), and when the reward is mis-specified (an energy rApp that "saves" power by degrading coverage). Hence the industry pattern: guard-railed AI — learned policies bounded by deterministic safety envelopes, and increasingly trained/validated inside digital twins before touching the live network. Which brings us to Section 20.

Slicing: an SLA that reaches the scheduler

A slice is an end-to-end logical network — its identity (S-NSSAI) rides from the core through transport into the RAN, where it must finally become something physical: a resource-partitioning policy in the MAC. Typical RAN slicing model: guaranteed PRB share + optional cap + priority class, e.g. "utility slice: ≥15% PRBs, latency class URLLC; consumer eMBB: best effort over the remainder; idle capacity borrowable." Core slicing gives dedicated UPF/SMF instances; transport slicing gives VPN/TSN lanes. The perennially hard part is SLA assurance — proving isolation held during the busy hour, which is precisely the per-slice analytics loop NWDAF + RIC apps are built for.

Same RAN, three tenants — a worked picture One n78 100 MHz TDD carrier at a port: (1) crane teleoperation slice — configured grants + mini-slots, 10 MHz-equivalent guaranteed, 5 ms budget; (2) camera/analytics slice — UL-heavy, 30% share, borrowable; (3) staff eMBB — best effort. The scheduler enforces all three every 0.5 ms; the twin verifies the SLA survives a traffic surge before the contract is signed.
22 · Energy efficiency

Watts Are the New Spectrum

Intuition

The RAN is usually the dominant contributor to a mobile operator's network energy — commonly cited as ~70–80% in many operator contexts, with radios consuming most of that. The exact share varies with operator, geography, site mix, cooling, legacy-network share and where you draw the accounting boundary, so treat it as a strong rule of thumb rather than a fixed constant. The crucial fact: a conventional site's power is dominated by load-independent terms — PAs biased on, transceivers powered, cooling running — so an idle cell still burns 50–70% of its peak power. Energy saving is therefore mostly about turning hardware off, gracefully, and predicting when you'll need it back.

24 · Non-terrestrial networks

When the Cell Tower Orbits at 7.5 km/s

Intuition

NTN inverts every terrestrial assumption. The "site" moves faster than any user (LEO ground track ~7.5 km/s), the propagation delay dwarfs the frame structure, and one beam covers a small country. NR-NTN (Rel-17+) kept the NR waveform but rebuilt the timing machinery around it.

What NTN forces onto the radio design

  • Timing: RTT up to ~540 ms (GEO) vs terrestrial µs. Fix: UEs use GNSS + broadcast ephemeris to pre-compensate delay and Doppler; HARQ gets huge (or disabled) process counts; scheduling offsets (K_offset) stretch the timeline.
  • Doppler: LEO at S-band ⇒ up to ~±48 kHz shift with fast drift — pre-compensated per beam center, residual handled by the UE.
  • Mobility inversion: "earth-moving" beams sweep users every few minutes even if nobody moves — handover becomes ephemeris-driven and predictable (conditional handover shines here).
  • Link budget: direct-to-device to an unmodified phone closes only with enormous satellite arrays (~100+ m² class) and low-band/S-band spectrum — hence messaging-first services (e.g., smartphone-to-satellite SOS/texting) before broadband.

Roles in the system: coverage extension (oceans, deserts, aviation), resilient backhaul for remote sites, IoT everywhere (Rel-17 IoT-NTN), and in 6G ambitions a genuinely integrated 3D network where terrestrial and orbital layers share spectrum, mobility management, and eventually regenerative on-board gNBs with inter-satellite laser mesh.

26 · Glossary

Working Vocabulary

AAU / RU — Active Antenna Unit / Radio Unit

The mast-top box converting digital samples ↔ RF. AAU integrates the antenna array and 32–64 transceivers for massive MIMO.

BLER / HARQ

Block Error Rate — fraction of transport blocks failing CRC. HARQ retransmits with soft combining; first-transmission BLER is deliberately targeted near 10%.

BWP — Bandwidth Part

A configured sub-slice of the carrier a UE operates in; enables power saving and device-capability tiering (RedCap).

CA / DSS

Carrier Aggregation bonds carriers across bands. Dynamic Spectrum Sharing time-multiplexes LTE and NR on one carrier during migration.

CQI / PMI / RI

The UE's channel report: quality index (→MCS), precoder matrix index (→beam/precoding), rank indicator (→number of MIMO layers).

CU / DU — Centralized / Distributed Unit

The gNB's split brain: CU runs PDCP/SDAP/RRC (ms-tolerant, centralizable); DU runs RLC/MAC/high-PHY (slot-deadline real time).

eCPRI / Open Fronthaul

Packet-based fronthaul carrying frequency-domain IQ between DU and RU (split 7.2x), with C/U/S/M planes; needs µs-class PTP synchronization.

ISAC / JCAS

Integrated Sensing and Communication — using the network's waveforms and infrastructure as a distributed radar while communicating; 6G's second product.

LDPC / Polar

NR's channel codes: LDPC for data (parallel, high-throughput, flexible rates), Polar for control (optimal at short blocks).

MCS — Modulation & Coding Scheme

The joint choice of QAM order and code rate per transmission — the output of link adaptation.

MU-MIMO / SU-MIMO

Spatial multiplexing across users vs to one user. MU-MIMO is the capacity multiplier of mid-band 5G; demands accurate CSI and spatially separated users.

Numerology (µ) / SCS

NR's scalable OFDM parameterization: SCS = 15·2^µ kHz; sets symbol/slot duration, CP length, latency floor, and Doppler tolerance.

NWDAF

The 5G core's analytics function — the standardized home for network-data ML on the core side (the RIC's counterpart in the RAN).

PRB / RE

Physical Resource Block (12 subcarriers) and Resource Element (1 subcarrier × 1 symbol) — the currency the scheduler spends.

RIC / xApp / rApp

RAN Intelligent Controllers: Near-RT RIC hosts xApps (10 ms–1 s control via E2); Non-RT RIC in the SMO hosts rApps (>1 s policy/training via A1/O1).

RSRP / RSRQ / SINR

Coverage (per-RE reference power), quality ratio (RSRP vs total received), and the ratio that actually determines throughput.

SBFD

Sub-band full duplex: gNB transmits and receives simultaneously in different sub-bands of one TDD carrier — a 5G-A/6G attack on the uplink bottleneck, gated by self-interference cancellation.

SSB / PRACH

The beam-swept sync/broadcast beacon a UE finds first, and the random-access channel it answers on — the front door of every connection.

SRS — Sounding Reference Signal

UL pilot that, via TDD reciprocity, reveals the DL channel — the fuel of reciprocity-based massive MIMO precoding.

S-NSSAI / 5QI

Slice identity and QoS class identifiers — how service contracts travel from core policy down to per-slot scheduler behavior.

UPF / AMF / SMF

The 5G core trio the RAN talks to: user-plane forwarding (placeable at the edge), access & mobility management, session management.

27 · Key takeaways

Ten Ideas That Organize Everything Else

  1. Four resources, one scheduler. Frequency, time, space, power — every RAN feature is a new way to allocate one of them.
  2. Latency budgets place hardware. HARQ pins the DU near the RU; PDCP frees the CU; URLLC pulls the UPF to the edge. Geography is a protocol consequence.
  3. Cloud ≠ centralized ≠ open. Implementation style, physical placement, and interface openness are three independent axes.
  4. Spectrum bands are roles, not speeds. Low = coverage layer, mid = workhorse, mmWave = capacity islands. A network is a portfolio.
  5. Massive MIMO is an information problem. The array is easy; fresh accurate CSI is the whole game — hence TDD reciprocity, SRS, and now AI-predicted channels.
  6. The uplink is the real edge. 0.2 W phones vs 200 W towers, plus TDD's DL-heavy duty cycle: most coverage and capacity crises are UL crises.
  7. Everything is a control loop with a timescale. Per-slot (in-DU) → 10 ms–1 s (xApps) → minutes+ (rApps). Match the intelligence to the loop it can actually close.
  8. Aggressive-plus-recovery beats cautious. HARQ at 10% BLER, PF riding fading peaks, sleep modes with fast wake: 3GPP design taste in one principle.
  9. Energy is mostly load-independent. Saving watts means switching hardware off — which makes energy efficiency a traffic-forecasting problem.
  10. 6G's bet is learned + sensing-native. 5G made networks programmable; 5G-A makes them learnable; 6G asks the network to perceive the world and design its own behavior — inside guard rails humans still set.

Suggested deep-dive order from here: SBFD self-interference budgets → cell-free fronthaul math → Rel-18 two-sided CSI model interoperability → ISAC capacity–distortion trade-off. Each is one question away.
